# UX Research Fundamentals

## Overview
UX Research systematically investigates user needs, behaviors, and motivations through observation and feedback to inform design decisions. This reference covers fundamental research methods, frameworks, and best practices.

## Core Concepts

### Concept 1: Research Types
Generative research (discovery) explores what users need and why. Evaluative research validates whether a design meets those needs. Attitudinal research asks what users say. Behavioral research observes what users do. Each type answers different questions.

### Concept 2: Qualitative vs Quantitative
Qualitative research (interviews, usability testing) answers "why" — rich insights from small samples. Quantitative research (surveys, analytics) answers "how many" — statistical patterns from large samples. Both are essential; neither replaces the other.

### Concept 3: Bias Mitigation
Common biases: confirmation bias (seeking evidence for assumptions), leading questions (influencing responses), social desirability (users saying what sounds good), recency bias (overweighting recent experiences), and self-reporting gap (difference between what users say and do).

### Concept 4: Synthesis
Raw research data must be synthesized into actionable insights: affinity diagrams (cluster observations), journey maps (visualize end-to-end experience), personas (archetypal users), problem statements (define focus). Synthesis transforms data into design direction.

### Concept 5: ResearchOps
ResearchOps is the infrastructure supporting research: participant recruitment, scheduling, consent forms, session recording, note-taking templates, insight repositories, and stakeholder communication. Good ResearchOps enables consistent, scalable research.

## Architecture Patterns

### Pattern 1: Double Diamond
Discover (research) → Define (synthesize) → Develop (ideate) → Deliver (test). Divergent thinking (exploring many options) alternates with convergent thinking (narrowing to one direction). Each phase uses different research methods.

### Pattern 2: Continuous Research Cadence
Weekly/bi-weekly usability tests with 3-5 participants. Monthly generative interviews. Quarterly surveys. Continuous analytics monitoring. Regular cadences prevent the "one big study" trap and build a culture of evidence-based design.

### Pattern 3: Hypothesis-Driven Research
Formulate hypotheses before each study: "We believe [user] needs [solution] because [evidence]." Design research to validate or invalidate specific hypotheses. This focuses research, reduces bias, and ties findings to decisions.

## Best Practices

- Research early and often (not just at project start)
- Triangulate methods (qual + quant for complete picture)
- Recruit representative participants (not coworkers, friends, or family)
- Use a discussion guide (never wing an interview)
- Record sessions (with consent) for stakeholder playback
- Synthesize immediately (insights fade)
- Share findings directly with the team
- Tie research to business outcomes

## Anti-Patterns

- Research as a one-time event at project start
- Testing only with ideal users (not edge cases)
- Only using surveys (misses "why")
- Only using interviews (misses "how many")
- Ignoring contradictory data (confirmation bias)
- Research without synthesis (data that never informs design)
- Over-relying on stakeholder opinions instead of user data
- Not involving the design team in research
