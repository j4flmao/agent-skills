# Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs)

## PIR Framework

### Defining PIRs
PIRs focus intelligence collection on what matters:
- **Critical**: Intelligence needed to prevent imminent attack or major breach
- **High**: Intelligence that significantly improves detection or response
- **Medium**: Intelligence that incrementally improves security posture
- **Low**: Nice-to-have intelligence, general awareness

### PIR Examples by Organization Type
| Organization | PIR Category | Example PIR |
|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Financial | Fraud | "What new payment fraud techniques are being used?" |
| Healthcare | Patient data | "Which ransomware groups are targeting healthcare?" |
| Tech/Software | Supply chain | "Which CI/CD vulnerabilities are being exploited?" |
| Government | Nation-state | "Which APT groups are targeting our sector?" |
| E-commerce | Payment card | "What new skimming techniques are observed?" |
| Critical infrastructure | OT/ICS | "Which ICS-targeting malware families are active?" |

## PIR Lifecycle
1. **Define**: Stakeholders agree on intelligence needs
2. **Validate**: Is the PIR answerable and actionable?
3. **Collect**: Focus intelligence gathering on PIRs
4. **Answer**: Produce intelligence that addresses PIRs
5. **Update**: PIRs evolve with threat landscape and business changes

## Key Points
- PIRs focus intelligence collection on what matters to your organization
- PIRs should be specific, answerable, and actionable
- Review and update PIRs quarterly based on threat landscape changes
- Each PIR should have an owner who validates the answer
- Share PIRs with intelligence providers to improve relevance
- Measure PIR answering rate to evaluate intelligence program effectiveness
