# Roadmap Fundamentals

## What is a Product Roadmap?
A product roadmap is a strategic document that communicates what the team is working on and why it matters. It aligns stakeholders around priorities, sets expectations about timing, and provides a shared direction for the product team.

## Core Concepts

### Strategic Themes
Themes are outcome-oriented initiatives that group related features. A good theme answers: "What will be true about the product when this work is complete?" Example: "Customers can manage their subscription online" instead of "Billing portal."

### Timeline Buckets
Communicate direction without over-committing to dates:
- **Now**: What is actively being worked on (this sprint/next sprint)
- **Next**: What is coming next (this quarter)
- **Later**: What is on the horizon (future quarters)

For mature products, use quarters with monthly milestones.

### Prioritization (RICE)
RICE = (Reach * Impact * Confidence) / Effort. Score every feature before placing it on the timeline. Higher RICE means ship it sooner.

### Capacity Allocation
A realistic roadmap accounts for all work, not just features:
- Feature work: 60%
- Tech debt: 20%
- Unplanned work (buffer): 20%

## The Three Essential Lists

### Must Have
What the team has committed to delivering. These are non-negotiable.

### Should Have
High-priority items that will be delivered if capacity allows. These are the next candidates.

### Won't Have (This Quarter)
Explicitly deferred items. This list is as important as the Must Have list — it sets boundaries.

## Rules

**Communicate direction, not delivery dates**: The roadmap answers "what and why" not "exactly when." Never promise specific feature dates unless contractually obligated.

**Reserve 20% buffer**: Every team has bugs, incidents, and escalations. Budget for them or watch the roadmap slip.

**Update monthly, never daily**: Daily changes destroy stakeholder trust. A monthly update cadence signals stability.

**Plan 4 quarters maximum**: Beyond 12 months is a vision document. The next two quarters should be the primary focus.

**Every theme maps to an OKR**: If a theme does not roll up to a business objective, it should not be on the roadmap.

## Common Mistakes

**Roadmap as a Gantt chart**: Exact dates create false certainty. Use month/quarter buckets.

**No "Won't Have" list**: Without explicit exclusions, stakeholders assume everything is possible.

**No capacity data**: A roadmap without capacity allocation is a wish list.

**Zero buffer**: Every team needs 20% buffer for unplanned work.

**Daily changes**: Frequent changes signal no strategy. Establish a monthly cadence.

**All operational themes**: If every theme is "improve performance," the product is not evolving.
