# Pitch Deck Advanced Topics

## Tailoring by Investor Type

### Angel Investors
Angels bet on people first. Emphasize founder-market fit — why this team is uniquely qualified to solve this problem. Personal founding stories create emotional connection. Market intuition and vision matter more than precise financial models.

### Seed VCs
Seed investors look for pattern matching. Is this a team that can execute? Is the market large enough? Early traction signals (waitlist numbers, pilot customers, early revenue) are powerful. Focus on the TAM story and team credentials.

### Series A VCs
Series A is about repeatability. Prove that the sales motion works, unit economics are sound, and the team can scale. LTV/CAC > 3, gross margin > 70%, payback period < 12 months. Cohort retention curves that flatline above zero.

### Series B+ VCs
Growth efficiency is the focus. Magic number (net new ARR / prior quarter sales & marketing spend), dollar-based net retention, payback period, burn multiple. The management team depth and path to profitability.

### Corporate/Strategic Investors
Different motivation — strategic value over financial return. Emphasize synergies, distribution partnerships, technology advantages, and how the startup fits within the corporate portfolio.

## Slide-Level Deep Dives

### Problem Slide
Use the "before and after" framing. Show the world without your product (painful, expensive, slow) and the world with your product (efficient, delightful, fast). A specific customer quote or case study is worth 10 bullet points.

### Market Slide
Layer the market story: top-down (TAM from analyst reports) + bottom-up (willingness to pay * target customers). The convergence of both methods is convincing. Include market trends — why is NOW the right time?

### Traction Slide
The traction curve should slope up and to the right. Highlight the growth rate, not just the absolute number. Include cohort retention to show quality, not just quantity. If possible, show a leading indicator that predicts future revenue.

### Competition Slide
Build a positioning map with 2 axes. Place competitors on the map. Show your unique position. Explain why the sweet spot you occupy is defensible. Include "do nothing" as a competitor.

### Business Model Slide
Show the unit economics clearly: CAC, LTV, gross margin, payback period. Explain the sales motion (self-serve, sales-led, channel). Include the customer acquisition strategy — how do you reach your target customers cost-effectively?

### Financial Slide
Show 3 scenarios: base case, conservative, optimistic. Highlight the key assumptions driving each scenario. The assumptions are more important than the numbers — investors scrutinize assumptions. Show the burn rate and runway.

### Ask Slide
Be specific: "We are raising a $2M seed round at a $10M pre-money valuation via a SAFE with a 20% discount and $15M cap." Include a use of funds breakdown: engineering (40%), go-to-market (35%), operations (15%), reserve (10%). State the expected runway (18 months).

## Handling Investor Questions

**"Why now?"**: Market timing is critical. What changed in technology, regulation, or consumer behavior that makes this the right moment?

**"What if a big company copies you?"**: Show your moat — network effects, data advantages, regulatory barriers, integration depth, brand, or speed of iteration.

**"What keeps you up at night?"**: Honest self-awareness is impressive. Name a real risk and explain your mitigation strategy.

**"How did you get your first 10 customers?"**: This tests your go-to-market understanding. Have a specific story for each channel.

**"What valuation are you expecting?"**: Be prepared with comparables (similar companies at similar stage) and your rationale.

## Visual Design Principles

- **Consistency**: Same fonts, colors, spacing, and positioning throughout
- **Hierarchy**: One element per slide should dominate visually
- **Contrast**: Use color and size to emphasize the one metric that matters
- **Whitespace**: Cramped slides signal unclear thinking
- **Data integrity**: Label axes, cite sources, use appropriate chart types

## Common Design Mistakes

- Chart junk: 3D effects, unnecessary gridlines, too many colors
- Inconsistent branding: Different logo placement, font changes, color shifts
- Data without context: A number without a comparison or baseline is meaningless
- Text-heavy visuals: Charts with too many data points or labels
- Low-quality images: Pixelated screenshots or photos undermine credibility

## Pre-Meeting Preparation
- Research each investor's portfolio, thesis, and recent activity
- Prepare a 1-page executive summary as a leave-behind
- Rehearse the deck without slides — if you need slides to tell the story, you do not know it
- Prepare answers to the top 10 questions investors will ask
- Know your numbers cold — no "I need to check" during the pitch

## Post-Meeting Follow-Up
- Send the deck within 2 hours of the meeting
- Address specific questions raised during the meeting in the follow-up email
- Include requested additional materials (financial model, product roadmap, customer references)
- Set clear next steps: "When should I follow up with you?"
