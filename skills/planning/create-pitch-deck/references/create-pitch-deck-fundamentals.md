# Pitch Deck Fundamentals

## What is a Pitch Deck?
A pitch deck is a brief presentation (typically 10 slides) used to communicate a business opportunity to potential investors. It tells a story: here is a problem, here is our solution, here is why we are the team to execute, and here is what we need from you.

## The 10-Slide Structure

### 1. Problem
Who is suffering, what is the pain, why is it urgent, why do existing solutions fail. Use a specific customer story or scenario — abstract problems are forgettable.

### 2. Solution
Your product and how it eliminates the pain. One memorable sentence. "Uber for X" is lazy — explain what makes your approach unique.

### 3. Market Size
TAM (total addressable market), SAM (serviceable addressable market), SOM (serviceable obtainable market). Each with a credible source and derivation. Investors need to believe the opportunity is large enough.

### 4. Product
Demo screenshots, user journey, magic moment. Show, do not tell. A live demo or realistic mockup is better than feature descriptions.

### 5. Traction
Metrics that prove the solution works. Revenue curve, user growth, cohort retention, NPS. Pre-revenue companies show early adopter engagement.

### 6. Business Model
Pricing, unit economics (CAC, LTV, payback period), sales channel, customer acquisition strategy. Investors need to understand how you make money.

### 7. Team
Founder backgrounds, relevant experience, prior exits, key hires, advisors. Why this team is uniquely positioned to win.

### 8. Competition
Positioning map, competitive moat, defensibility. Acknowledge competitors honestly and explain your differentiation.

### 9. Financials
3-5 year projection, expense breakdown, key assumptions, burn rate. Be realistic — hockey-stick projections undermine credibility.

### 10. Ask
Specific amount, valuation, instrument type, use of funds breakdown, expected runway. "We are raising a $2M seed round at $10M pre-money."

## The Storytelling Arc

### Hook (Slides 1-2)
Grab attention with a relatable problem. Make the audience feel the pain.

### Tension (Slides 3-4)
Reveal the magnitude of the opportunity. The market is enormous and the product is real.

### Resolution (Slides 5-7)
Present proof: traction, business model, team. Show that you can execute.

### Vision (Slides 8-10)
Paint the future and make the ask. This is where the investor becomes a partner.

## Key Rules

**One message per slide**: If a slide has two distinct ideas, split it. A slide with two messages communicates none.

**30 words max per slide**: Body text under 30 words. Speaker notes carry details. Slides should be glanceable in 3 seconds.

**Visuals beat text**: Every slide needs a visual — chart, screenshot, diagram, photo. Wall-of-bullets decks are the #1 anti-pattern.

**One metric that matters per slide**: Feature the single number the audience must remember.

**End each slide with a hook**: The last element should create curiosity for the next slide.

## Common Mistakes

**Feature dump**: Listing features instead of benefits. Investors invest in outcomes.

**Generic market size**: "$100B TAM" without credible sourcing. Derive your SAM and SOM.

**No competition**: Claiming no competitors signals naivety. Every product has competition.

**Vague ask**: "Looking for funding" is not an ask. Be specific about amount and terms.

**Too much text**: Walls of text make the audience read instead of listen. Keep it visual.

**Wrong emphasis for stage**: Seed investors want team and vision. Series A wants traction and unit economics. Series B+ wants growth efficiency and market dominance.
