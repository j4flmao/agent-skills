# Interview Rubrics

## Overview
Rubrics provide the objective framework for candidate evaluation.
Each dimension is scored 1-4 with behavioral anchors.
The rubric is the same for every candidate — never adjusted per person.
Rubrics ensure consistent, bias-resistant evaluation across interviewers.

## Scoring Scale
| Score | Label | Definition |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Strong No | Below expectations — clear gap, would not hire |
| 2 | No | Meets some but not enough to advance |
| 3 | Yes | Meets expectations — strong enough to hire |
| 4 | Strong Yes | Exceeds expectations — exceptional, rare |

A score of 4 is rare — genuinely exceptional, not just good.
A score of 1 means a clear specific gap that won't close with ramp-up.
Every interviewer must calibrate their scoring against team average.

## Coding Rubric

| Dimension | 1 (Strong No) | 2 (No) | 3 (Yes) | 4 (Strong Yes) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Problem-solving | No clear approach, jumps to code | Basic approach, needs hints | Systematic, explores tradeoffs | Elegant, optimizes and analyzes |
| Communication | Can't explain reasoning | Explains with prompting | Clear explanation throughout | Teaches interviewer, asks clarifying questions |
| Code quality | Unreadable, no structure | Functional but messy | Clean, idiomatic, well-structured | Production-quality, considers edge cases |
| Testing awareness | No testing consideration | Basic happy path only | Covers edge cases and error states | TDD approach, testability built in |

### Coding Guidelines
Provide clear problem statement with examples.
Let candidate ask clarifying questions before starting.
Language choice is their preference with environment setup.

Time management: 5 min clarification, 25 min implementation,
10 min testing and optimization, 5 min candidate questions.

Common mistakes: skipping clarification phase, optimizing too early,
not testing the solution with edge cases, poor time management.
Evaluate approach over code quality for senior roles.

## System Design Rubric

| Dimension | 1 (Strong No) | 2 (No) | 3 (Yes) | 4 (Strong Yes) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Requirements | No clarifying questions | Basic clarification only | Systematic gathering, functional + non-functional | Challenges assumptions, prioritizes, defines MVP |
| Architecture | No coherent design | Simple but functional | Well-structured with clear components and flows | Distributed, fault-tolerant, evolvable |
| Scalability | No consideration | Vague scaling mentions | Concrete: sharding, caching, CDN, queues | Quantitative with tradeoff data and bottlenecks |
| Tradeoffs | None mentioned | One when prompted | Multiple with rationale | Quantitative comparison of alternatives |
| Data model | Not defined | Simple but incomplete | Well-defined schema, access patterns | Optimized for read/write ratio, indexing strategy |

### System Design Guidelines
Scope the problem: DAU, data volume, read/write ratio, latency requirements.
Start high-level architecture, then dive into specific components.
Draw architecture diagram. Discuss tradeoffs explicitly — no perfect design.

Time: 10 min requirements, 20 min design, 10 min deep dive, 5 min tradeoffs.

## Behavioral Rubric

| Dimension | 1 (Strong No) | 2 (No) | 3 (Yes) | 4 (Strong Yes) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Collaboration | Blames others, no teamwork examples | Works alone but can collaborate when needed | Strong team player, unblocks others, seeks diverse perspectives | Elevates team, mentors, creates inclusive environment |
| Ownership | Blames circumstances, no accountability | Takes ownership when asked | Proactively owns outcomes, follows through | Goes beyond scope, drives cross-functional results |
| Growth mindset | Resists feedback, defensive | Accepts but doesn't act on it | Seeks feedback, improves, learns from failure | Teaches others, creates learning culture |
| Communication | Unclear, rambling, can't tailor | Gets point across but not structured | Clear, concise, adjusts to audience | Persuasive, inspires action, handles difficult conversations |

### Behavioral Guidelines
Use STAR method: Situation, Task, Action, Result.
Ask for specific examples, not hypothetical scenarios.
Probe for depth: "What was your specific role?", "What did you do differently?",
"What was the outcome?", "What would you do differently now?"

Listen for self-awareness, impact on outcomes, and learning from mistakes.
Avoid "we" without clarifying the candidate's individual role.
Avoid leading questions that suggest the desired answer.
Ask about failure and conflict explicitly — how they handle adversity.
Take notes on specific quotes and observations, not general impressions.

## Calibration
Every 3-6 months, calibrate rubrics across the interviewer team.
Use example candidates (recordings or mock interviews) to align scores.
Flag interviewers scoring consistently above or below team average by over 0.5.

New interviewers: shadow 3 sessions, co-interview 3 sessions before solo.
Calibration is continuous — update rubrics as you learn new signals.

Document calibration sessions: which dimensions had disagreement,
how they were resolved, updated anchor descriptions.

## Key Points
Rubric dimensions are non-negotiable — score every dimension for every candidate.
Behavioral anchors prevent arbitrary scoring — use the descriptions in each cell.
A score of 4 should be rare — it means genuinely exceptional.
Calibration sessions prevent drift — without them, rubrics lose value.
Document specific evidence for each score: quotes and observations, not just a number.
Rubrics should evolve as the role evolves — review and update annually.
Every dimension must have clear, non-overlapping definitions.
Share rubric with candidates before the interview for transparency.
Score immediately after each interview — don't rely on memory.
Discuss discrepancies as a hiring committee before making final decisions.
