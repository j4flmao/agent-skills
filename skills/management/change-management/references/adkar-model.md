# ADKAR Model

## Overview
ADKAR is a goal-oriented change management model by Prosci that focuses on five individual outcomes necessary for successful change. It's used both to diagnose gaps and to plan interventions.

## The Five Elements

### Awareness (of the need for change)
Goal: Individuals understand why the change is happening, what is driving it, and the risk of not changing.
Key questions: What is the change and why is it happening? What is the business driver behind the change? What happens if we don't change? What is the personal risk of not changing?

Assessment: Score 1-5 per stakeholder group. 1 = No awareness at all. 2 = Heard something but unclear. 3 = Understands the reason. 4 = Can explain it to others. 5 = Actively advocates for the need.
Gap interventions: Town halls, leadership messages, one-on-one discussions, Q&A sessions, case studies of similar changes.

Common pitfalls: Assuming one communication is enough. Telling the "what" without the "why." Not connecting to personal impact. Using jargon instead of plain language.

### Desire (to participate and support the change)
Goal: Individuals make a personal choice to support the change and engage with it.
Key questions: What's in it for me (WIIFM)? Why should I support this? What will I lose or gain? Do I trust the leadership driving this change?

Assessment: Score 1-5 per stakeholder group. 1 = Actively resistant. 2 = Skeptical but could be persuaded. 3 = Neutral, willing to see. 4 = Supportive. 5 = Champion.
Gap interventions: Engage influencers and respected peers. Address personal impact directly. Connect change to organizational values. Create peer success stories. Involve resisters in solution design.

Common pitfalls: Ignoring resisters. Assuming rational arguments alone convince. Not addressing fear of loss. Lack of visible sponsor commitment.

### Knowledge (of how to change)
Goal: Individuals have the information, training, and understanding needed to implement the change.
Key questions: What specific skills are needed? What training is available? How do I learn the new way? What resources exist to help? Who do I go to for help?

Assessment: Score 1-5 per stakeholder group. 1 = No idea what to do. 2 = Knows what but not how. 3 = Has basic training. 4 = Can perform independently. 5 = Can teach others.
Gap interventions: Role-specific training, hands-on workshops, job aids and cheat sheets, mentorship programs, peer learning groups, sandbox environments.

Common pitfalls: One-size-fits-all training. Training too early or too late. No ongoing support after training. Training on systems without context. Assuming training = ability.

### Ability (to implement the change day-to-day)
Goal: Individuals can effectively apply the knowledge to achieve the desired performance.
Key questions: Can they do it in their real work environment? What barriers exist? Do they have the time and tools? Is the environment supportive?

Assessment: Score 1-5 per stakeholder group. 1 = Unable to perform. 2 = Can perform with heavy support. 3 = Can perform with occasional support. 4 = Performs independently. 5 = Mentors others.
Gap interventions: Coaching and mentoring, process simplification, tool improvements, time allocation for practice, remove system barriers, buddy system.

Knowledge vs Ability gap: knowledge is knowing, ability is doing. A person may have completed training (knowledge) but cannot perform in real conditions (ability). This is the most common ADKAR gap — underinvesting in ability after training.

### Reinforcement (to sustain the change)
Goal: Mechanisms exist to ensure the change sticks and doesn't revert to old ways.
Key questions: How do we prevent backsliding? Are managers reinforcing the change? Are systems aligned with the new way? Do we celebrate successes? Do we correct regression?

Assessment: Score 1-5 per stakeholder group. 1 = No reinforcement. 2 = Inconsistent recognition. 3 = Some celebrations and corrections. 4 = Regular reinforcement in place. 5 = Change is embedded in culture.
Gap interventions: Recognition and rewards, performance management alignment, metrics and dashboards, leadership visibility, celebration of wins, consequence for regression, process audits, continuous improvement.

## Assessment Method

### Individual Assessment
Assess each person on all five elements. Use surveys, interviews, or observation. Score 1-5 per element. Identify the lowest score — that's the current blocker. Address blockers in order (A before D, D before K, etc.). Cannot skip elements — each builds on previous.

### Group Assessment
Aggregate scores by stakeholder group. Identify group-level patterns. Plan interventions for groups, customized by their blocker element. Compare groups to prioritize change effort investment.

### Organizational Assessment
Roll up group scores for organizational view. Identify systemic gaps across all groups. Allocate resources to largest gaps first. Track score improvement over time.

## Application Process

### Diagnose
Measure ADKAR scores for each stakeholder group. Identify which element is the primary blocker. Use that diagnosis to design interventions. Reassess after interventions.

### Design
Target interventions at the lowest-scoring element. Awareness → communications and leadership messages. Desire → engagement and personal connection. Knowledge → training and documentation. Ability → coaching, tools, and environment. Reinforcement → systems, metrics, rewards.

### Implement
Execute interventions in ADKAR order. Don't try to build Desire before Awareness is established. Don't train (Knowledge) before Desire is present. Transition from group interventions to individual support as needed.

### Evaluate
Reassess ADKAR scores after intervention. Track improvement per element. Identify next blocker and repeat cycle. Continue until adoption targets are met. Monitor Reinforcement for at least 6 months.

## Key Points
ADKAR is individual — assess per person or stakeholder group, not organization-wide.
Elements must be built in order — A → D → K → A → R.
Lowest score is the bottleneck — address it first.
Knowledge without ability is the most common gap — invest in practice.
Reinforcement is the most neglected element — plan for it upfront.
ADKAR can diagnose and plan, not just measure.
