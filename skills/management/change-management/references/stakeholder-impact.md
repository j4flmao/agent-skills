# Stakeholder Impact Analysis

## Overview
Stakeholder impact analysis identifies who is affected by a change, how much they are impacted, and their readiness to adopt. This data drives communication, resistance management, training, and support planning.

## Stakeholder Mapping

### Power/Interest Grid
Plot each stakeholder group on a 2x2 grid based on their power over the change and their interest in it. High Power + High Interest: key players, engage actively, involve in design. High Power + Low Interest: keep satisfied, provide executive summaries. Low Power + High Interest: keep informed, leverage as champions or influencers. Low Power + Low Interest: monitor, minimal investment.

### Impact Dimensions
Process change: how much does daily workflow change? Technology change: new tools or systems to learn? Role change: responsibilities, reporting, or authority changes? Cultural change: values, norms, or behaviors expected to shift? Location change: physical or team move? Assess each dimension per stakeholder group: 1 = no impact, 5 = complete transformation.

### Readiness Assessment
For each stakeholder group assess: Awareness: do they know the change is coming? Desire: are they motivated to participate? Knowledge: do they have the needed information? Ability: can they perform in the new state? Reinforcement: will support systems sustain the change? Score each 1-5. Composite readiness = average of all five.

### Matrix Integration
Plot each group on a 2x2: impact (high/low) vs readiness (high/low). High Impact + Low Readiness: highest risk, most attention and support needed. This is where change management effort concentrates. High Impact + High Readiness: leverage as champions and peer influencers. Low Impact + Low Readiness: provide basic support, minimal investment. Low Impact + High Readiness: leave alone, they will adapt independently.

## Change Readiness Assessment

### Assessment Methods
Surveys: quantitative readiness scores per group. Focus groups: qualitative insights into concerns and barriers. Interviews: deep dive with key stakeholders. Observation: behavioral indicators of readiness. Past change performance: how did this group handle previous changes?

### Readiness Dimensions
Understanding: do they comprehend the change and its rationale? Alignment: does the change align with their values and interests? Capability: do they have or can they develop the needed skills? Trust: do they trust leadership to manage the change effectively? Capacity: do they have bandwidth to engage with the change? Score each 1-5 and track over time.

### Readiness Improvement
Low understanding → targeted communications and Q&A. Low alignment → connect to personal motivations, identify WIIFM. Low capability → training and skill-building programs. Low trust → visible sponsor commitment, transparency, listening. Low capacity → reduce other initiatives, provide time and resources.

## Resistance Management

### Resistance Sources
Fear of loss: job security, status, competence, relationships. Lack of trust: history of failed changes, untrusted leadership. Misunderstanding: unclear rationale, conflicting messages. Low tolerance for change: past trauma, personal disposition. Competing priorities: too many changes simultaneously. Not invented here: excluded from design, feel imposed upon.

### Resistance Types
Active resistance: vocal opposition, organizing against, refusing to adopt. Passive resistance: agreeing publicly but not changing behavior, foot-dragging, quiet quitting. Resistance by compliance: doing exactly what's asked but no more, not using judgment. Each type requires different intervention.

### Resistance Management Strategies
Active resistance: one-on-one dialogue, address specific concerns, involve in solution design, clear consequences. Passive resistance: surface unspoken concerns, model expected behavior, peer influence, accountability conversations. Early adopters: amplify their voice, make their success visible, reward advocacy. Undecided: provide evidence, peer stories, low-risk trials.

## Communication Planning

### Per-Group Plan
Each stakeholder group needs: message tailored to their concerns and impact, channel appropriate for their context, cadence matching their need for information, owner responsible for delivery, feedback mechanism for two-way dialogue.

### Message Content
What is changing (specific, concrete). Why it's changing (business rationale, drivers). When it's changing (timeline, key milestones). How it affects you (personal impact, role changes). What you need to do (specific actions). Where to get help (support resources, contacts).

### Communication Channels
Email: formal announcements, documentation, reference materials.
Town hall: big news, vision, leadership visibility.
Team meetings: local context, Q&A, practical impact.
One-on-one: sensitive topics, personal impact, resistance management.
Slack/Teams: quick updates, informal communications, peer discussion.
Newsletter: regular progress updates, success stories, tips.

### Cadence
Pre-announcement (2-4 weeks before): teaser, context, prepare. Launch (day of): full details, training available, support ready. First 30 days: weekly updates, success stories, address issues. 30-90 days: biweekly updates, metrics, continuous improvement. 90+ days: monthly updates, reinforcement, celebration.

## Training Needs Assessment

### Assessment Questions
What new knowledge is required? What new skills are required? What existing skills can be leveraged? What is the current proficiency level? What is the target proficiency level? What is the best learning format for this group? What support is needed post-training?

### Training Design
Role-specific: tailor to each group's new responsibilities. Hands-on: practice in safe environment before real work. Just-in-time: deliver when needed, not weeks before. Multi-modal: mix of self-paced, instructor-led, peer learning. Continuous: support beyond the initial training event.

### Support Structure
Tier 1: self-service (documentation, FAQs, video tutorials). Tier 2: peer support (super users, champions, buddies). Tier 3: expert support (trainers, coaches, help desk). Each tier should resolve the majority of issues before escalating.

## Key Points
High impact + low readiness groups need the most investment.
Resistance is data — understand the root cause before intervening.
Communication must be two-way, relevant, and frequent.
Training without practice is knowledge without ability — both are needed.
Each stakeholder group needs a unique plan, not a one-size-fits-all approach.
Readiness is dynamic — reassess at each change phase.
Leverage early adopters as champions — peer influence beats top-down.
