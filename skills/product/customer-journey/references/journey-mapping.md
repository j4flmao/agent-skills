# Journey Mapping

## Overview
Journey mapping is a visual representation of a customer's experience across lifecycle stages and touchpoints with a product or service. Maps reveal pain points, moments of truth, and opportunities for improvement.

## Customer Lifecycle Stages

### Awareness
Customer discovers the product or brand exists.
Touchpoints: ads, social media, word of mouth, search, content, reviews.
Key questions: Who are you? What do you offer? Why should I care?
Metrics: reach, impressions, traffic sources, brand search volume.
Pain points: unclear value proposition, irrelevant targeting, poor SEO.

### Consideration
Customer evaluates the product against alternatives.
Touchpoints: website, pricing page, case studies, demos, trials, reviews.
Key questions: Does this solve my problem? Is it worth the cost? Is it better than alternatives?
Metrics: page depth, time on site, demo requests, trial starts, feature exploration.
Pain points: confusing pricing, missing features, long sales process, weak differentiation.

### Conversion
Customer commits and becomes a paying user.
Touchpoints: signup flow, checkout, payment, onboarding.
Key questions: Is it easy to get started? Is my data safe? What happens next?
Metrics: conversion rate, signup completion, payment success, time to first action.
Pain points: complex forms, hidden fees, payment errors, delayed activation.

### Retention
Customer continues using the product over time.
Touchpoints: product experience, support, notifications, updates.
Key questions: Is it still working for me? Am I getting value? Do they care about me?
Metrics: DAU/MAU, session frequency, feature adoption, churn rate, support tickets.
Pain points: degraded performance, unresponsive support, irrelevant notifications, feature stagnation.

### Advocacy
Customer actively promotes the product to others.
Touchpoints: referral programs, reviews, social sharing, community.
Key questions: Would I recommend this? Do I feel loyal? Do they appreciate my loyalty?
Metrics: NPS, referral rate, review ratings, organic mentions, community participation.
Pain points: no referral incentive, difficult sharing, ignored feedback, no loyalty recognition.

## Touchpoint Mapping

### Touchpoint Categories
Digital: website, mobile app, email, chat, social media, help center.
Physical: store, kiosk, packaging, event, printed materials.
Human: sales call, support call, onboarding session, meeting.
Service: billing, provisioning, account management, training.

### Mapping Method
List every interaction point across the full lifecycle.
Categorize by channel type and ownership.
Rate each on: importance to customer (1-5), current satisfaction (1-5), effort level (1-5).
Identify channel transitions (e.g., website to phone call) and their smoothness.
Flag orphan touchpoints no team owns.

## Moments of Truth

### Definition
A moment of truth is any interaction where the customer forms or changes an impression about the brand. These disproportionately impact overall satisfaction and loyalty.

### Types
First Moment of Truth: when customer first encounters the product (shelf, search result, ad). Zero Moment of Truth: when customer researches before purchase (reviews, social, content). Second Moment of Truth: when customer uses the product (first experience, onboarding). Ultimate Moment of Truth: when customer shares experience (review, referral, complaint).

### Identification Method
Analyze CSAT data for high-variance touchpoints (some love, some hate). Look for emotional spikes in journey research sessions. Identify where effort scores are highest. Find touchpoints mentioned most in churn surveys.

### Assessment
Rate each moment: current satisfaction, impact on overall experience, improvement feasibility.
Prioritize: low satisfaction + high impact = urgent action.
High satisfaction + high impact = protect and maintain.
Low satisfaction + low impact = monitor.
High satisfaction + low impact = no action.

## Pain Points

### Categories
Usability: hard to use, confusing, slow, broken.
Information: unclear, missing, contradictory, too much.
Service: slow response, unhelpful, impersonal, hard to reach.
Process: long steps, repetitive, stuck in loops, no progress.
Value: too expensive, not worth it, doesn't solve problem.

### Analysis
For each pain point: which lifecycle stage, which touchpoint, severity (how bad), frequency (how often), customer impact (churn risk, CSAT drop).
Prioritization matrix: severity × frequency = priority score.
Validate pain points with actual customer data, not assumptions.

## Current-State vs Future-State Maps
Current-state map documents the real experience today, including workarounds and failures.
Future-state map designs the ideal experience, removing friction and adding value.
Create future-state from pain points and opportunities in current-state.

## Mapping Workshop Facilitation
Invite cross-functional participants: product, design, engineering, support, sales.
Prepare journey scope, existing data, customer research ahead.
Use large physical or digital canvas. Start with customer actions, add emotions, then touchpoints, then internal processes.
Mark pain points, moments of truth, and opportunities as they emerge.

## Key Points
Always map from customer perspective, not internal org structure.
Validate maps with actual customer research, not team assumptions.
Include emotional journey — what customer feels at each step.
Journey maps are hypotheses until validated with data and research.
Update maps quarterly or when significant product changes occur.
