# Customer Journey Fundamentals

## Overview
Customer journey mapping visualizes the end-to-end experience a customer has with a product or service — from initial awareness through ongoing usage and advocacy. By understanding each touchpoint, the customer's emotional state, and the moments that matter most, teams can identify friction points, optimize experiences, and build products that customers love.

## Core Concepts

### Concept 1: Journey vs Funnel
A journey map is a qualitative, customer-centric narrative of the complete experience. A funnel is a quantitative, business-centric view of conversion through defined stages. Journeys reveal WHY customers behave a certain way (emotions, motivations, context). Funnels reveal WHAT customers do (actions, drop-offs, conversion rates). Both are needed: funnels tell you where to look, journeys tell you what to do when you get there.

### Concept 2: Touchpoints and Channels
A touchpoint is any interaction between customer and brand (viewing an ad, visiting a website, calling support, receiving an email). A channel is the medium of that interaction (web, mobile, email, chat, phone, in-person). Customers interact through multiple channels — the quality of cross-channel experience often determines overall satisfaction more than any single touchpoint.

### Concept 3: Emotional Journey
Customers don't just follow steps — they feel emotions at each stage. Mapping the emotional timeline reveals where customers feel frustrated, anxious, delighted, or confused. The emotional journey often has more impact on loyalty and churn than the functional journey. A functionally perfect experience can fail if it feels impersonal or stressful.

### Concept 4: Moments of Truth
Moments of truth are critical touchpoints that disproportionately impact overall experience perception. The first impression (first visit or onboarding), the problem moment (when something goes wrong and support is needed), and the peak moment (the most intense positive or negative experience) have outsized impact on how customers remember their journey.

### Concept 5: Current-State vs Future-State
Current-state journey maps document the existing experience — what customers actually do, including workarounds and frustrations. Future-state journey maps visualize the ideal experience — what it should feel like after improvements. Never design a future-state without first understanding the current-state — assumptions about the current experience are often wrong.

## Journey Mapping

### Map Structure
Organize the journey as a timeline across lifecycle stages: Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Onboarding → Usage → Support → Advocacy. For each stage, document: customer actions, touchpoints, channels, goals, questions, emotions, pain points, and opportunities.

### Data Sources for Journey Maps
- **Customer research:** interviews, diary studies, contextual inquiry — reveals WHY
- **Analytics:** funnel data, path analysis, feature usage — reveals WHAT
- **Support data:** ticket volume by topic, CSAT by touchpoint — reveals PAIN
- **Survey data:** NPS, CES, CSAT by stage — reveals SATISFACTION
- **Session recordings:** actual user behavior — reveals WORKAROUNDS

### Emotional Timeline
Plot customer emotion (1-10) across the journey timeline. Identify peaks (delight) and valleys (frustration). Valleys are optimization opportunities. Peaks should be protected and replicated. Compare emotional journey across segments — different personas may have different emotional responses at the same touchpoint.

## Service Blueprinting

### Blueprint Layers
Extend journey maps by showing the operational reality behind the customer experience:
- **Physical evidence:** every artifact the customer encounters (emails, invoices, screens)
- **Customer actions:** what the customer does at each step
- **Frontstage actions:** visible employee or system interactions with the customer
- **Backstage actions:** invisible work that supports the frontstage
- **Support processes:** underlying systems, tools, and third parties

### Three Lines
- **Line of interaction:** separates customer actions from provider actions
- **Line of visibility:** separates what customers see from what happens behind the scenes
- **Line of internal interaction:** separates core team from support teams

### Blueprint Analysis
For each customer action, verify there is at least one supporting frontstage or backstage action and a support process. Mark fail points with severity and frequency estimates. Identify wait times — both explicit (customer perceives the wait) and implicit (processing time customer does not see). Flag process gaps where no defined process exists for a customer expectation.

## Touchpoint Analysis

### Touchpoint Audit
List every touchpoint in the current journey. Rate each on: importance to customer (1-5), current satisfaction (1-5), effort required (1-5), consistency across channels (1-5). Calculate touchpoint health score: satisfaction × importance / effort. Prioritize touchpoints with low health scores for improvement.

### Channel Transition Analysis
Identify every point where customers switch channels (web to phone, email to chat, mobile to desktop). For each transition, assess: is context preserved? Does the customer repeat information? Is there a delay? Cross-channel failures are some of the most damaging experience issues because they force customers to work harder.

## Key Points
- Journey maps are customer narratives, not process flows — always start from the customer perspective
- Emotional journey often matters more than functional journey for loyalty
- Moments of truth have outsized impact — identify and optimize them first
- Validate every journey assumption with research and data
- Current-state must be understood before designing future-state
- Service blueprints extend journey maps with operational reality
- Every customer action needs supporting frontstage, backstage, and process layers
- Channel transitions are common failure points — design them carefully
- Cross-functional involvement is essential for accurate service blueprints
- Journey maps are hypotheses until validated with data and research
- Time-to-value is a leading indicator of retention — include in all journey analyses
- CES predicts churn more accurately than satisfaction — focus on effort reduction
