# Empathy Mapping

## Overview
An empathy map is a collaborative visualization that articulates what a persona says, thinks, does, and feels. It builds deep user understanding, surfaces needs users may not explicitly state, and aligns the team around a shared, research-grounded mental model of the user.

## Empathy Map Template

### The Four Quadrants

Says: what the persona explicitly states aloud. Direct quotes from interviews, support calls, or survey open-ended responses. Include verbatim quotes, not paraphrases. "I need a simpler dashboard" not "They want a better dashboard."

Thinks: what the persona is thinking but may not say. Unvoiced concerns, private doubts, unspoken needs. Infer from behavior, context, and tone. Often contradicts what they say. "This is probably too expensive" but they say "I need to check my budget."

Does: observable actions and behaviors. What they actually do, not what they say they do. How they navigate the product, what they click, where they spend time. Their workarounds and habits. Actions can contradict words — behavior reveals true intent. "Opens competitor site after every demo" reveals consideration stage behavior.

Feels: emotional state associated with the experience. Frustration at complexity, anxiety about data loss, delight at speed. Surface emotions from tone of voice, word choice, and body language (in recorded sessions). Emotions drive loyalty and churn more than features do.

### Pains
What frustrates, blocks, or worries the persona. Fears about losing data or making mistakes. Obstacles preventing goal achievement. Annoyances with current solution. Negative emotions associated with the task. Pain magnitude: how bad is it and how often does it occur?

### Gains
What the persona aspires to achieve. Desired outcomes, success criteria. Dreams about what the product could do for them. Measures of success from their perspective. Positive emotions they want to feel: confident, relieved, productive, respected.

## Empathy Map Construction

### Workshop Setup
Large whiteboard or digital canvas divided into the four quadrants plus pains and gains. Have sticky notes in different colors per quadrant. Start with the persona name and photo at center. Display research data (quotes, clips, notes) visible to all participants. Include 4-8 cross-functional participants.

### Facilitation Steps
Draw the empathy map template on canvas with the persona at center.
Start with Says: "What have we heard this persona say? Use actual quotes from research."
Move to Does: "What does this persona actually do? What behaviors have we observed?"
Go to Thinks: "What might this persona be thinking but not saying? What's beneath the surface?"
End with Feels: "What emotions does this persona experience? Where are the emotional peaks and valleys?"
Complete pains and gains: "What frustrates them? What do they aspire to?"

### Quality Checks
Is each item grounded in research, not assumption? Are says and thinks differentiated (stated vs unstated)? Does each quadrant have at least 5 items? Do the quadrants tell a coherent story? Are there contradictions between quadrants that reveal insight? Is the persona name used consistently?

## Persona Empathy

### Building Empathy
The goal is not just to describe the user but to feel what they feel. Read persona quotes aloud in team meetings. Watch recorded research sessions as a team. Walk through the user journey and say what the persona would be thinking at each step. Roleplay scenarios from the persona's perspective. Use the empathy map as a decision filter: "Would Sarah feel this is helpful?"

### Common Empathy Failures
Designing for yourself: "I would use it this way" — you are not the user.
Stereotyping: "All marketers want X" — personas are specific, not generic.
Projecting: "They must feel Y" — says and thinks must come from research.
Ignoring says-thinks gap: when words and behavior conflict, trust behavior.

## Pain/Gain Mapping

### Pain Mapping
List all pains per persona. Categorize: functional (task-related), emotional (feelings), social (perception, reputation). Rate severity and frequency. Prioritize: high severity + high frequency = urgent. Connect each pain to a specific journey stage or touchpoint. Validate with research — not all assumed pains are real.

### Gain Mapping
List all desired outcomes per persona. Distinguish between expressed desires ("I want more reports") and underlying needs ("I want to feel in control"). Identify which gains are unmet by current solutions. Prioritize gains by importance to the persona. Map gains to product features or experiences.

## Persona Validation

### Validation Methods
Research interviews: present persona description, ask if it rings true. Surveys: test persona attributes at scale with broader audience. Analytics: does behavioral data match persona assumptions? Support review: do support patterns align with persona pain points? A/B test: does persona-specific messaging outperform generic?

### Validation Criteria
At least 3 research participants match each persona pattern. Behavioral data supports the assumed goals and pain points. Stakeholders recognize real customers in the persona. The persona helps predict user behavior and preferences. Team members reference the persona in design decisions.

### When to Update
New research reveals different patterns. Product pivots to different market. User behavior shifts significantly. User base expands to new segments. Existing persona no longer helps predict behavior.

## Key Points
Says and thinks are different — the gap reveals deep insights.
Behavior (does) is more reliable than self-report (says).
Emotions drive churn more than features — map them carefully.
Connect each pain to a specific journey stage.
Validate empathy maps with actual user research.
Empathy maps are a tool for decision-making, not a deliverable.
