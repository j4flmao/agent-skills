# Persona Creation

## Overview
A persona is a research-backed archetype representing a user segment with shared goals, behaviors, and pain points. Personas align teams around user needs, prevent self-referential design, and enable prioritization decisions grounded in user value.

## Persona Types

### Primary Persona
The main target user whose needs must be met for the product to succeed. Every feature decision should be tested against this persona. If a feature doesn't serve the primary persona, deprioritize it. Design primarily for this persona; others should not suffer, but primary gets priority. Example: "Sarah, the busy marketing manager who needs quick campaign reporting."

### Secondary Persona
Important user who has additional or slightly conflicting needs. Accommodated without compromising primary persona experience. May need alternative flows or settings. Served by after-thought features or extensions. Example: "Alex, the CMO who needs executive dashboards across campaigns."

### Anti-Persona
Explicitly NOT a target user. Documented to prevent scope creep and maintain focus. Feature requests from anti-persona should be politely declined. Example: "Developer who wants API access and custom integrations — our product is no-code for marketers." Helps teams say no with evidence instead of intuition.

### Negative Persona
A user who would actively harm the product. Fraudsters, abusers, bad-fit customers. Design safeguards against, not for, this type. Security rules, terms of service, fraud detection.

### Supplemental Persona
Stakeholder who isn't an end user but influences purchasing. Buyer personas for B2B: economic buyer, technical evaluator, champion, influencer. Their needs differ from end user needs. Sales cycles often serve buyer personas more than user personas.

## Data Sources

### Primary Research
User interviews: 8-15 per segment, 45-60 minutes each, semi-structured. Contextual inquiry: observe users in their natural environment. Diary studies: users log activities over days or weeks.

### Secondary Research
Product analytics: behavioral cohorts, feature usage patterns. Support ticket analysis: categorize pain points by user type. Sales call notes: what prospects ask about, objection patterns. Survey data: large-scale preferences, satisfaction scores, demographics.

### Data Synthesis
Gather all raw data. Sort observations into clusters by similarity. Label clusters with emerging themes. Look for behavioral patterns across demographics, not just job titles. Identify 3-5 distinct persona clusters where behavior and goals meaningfully differ.

## Persona Template

### Name and Tagline
Fictional name with a descriptive tagline.
"Sarah — The busy marketing manager who needs dashboards, not data dumps."

### Demographics
Role, title, experience level, company size, industry, location.
Only include if relevant to product decisions.

### Goals and Motivations
What does this persona want to achieve? What motivates them? What does success look like to them? Distinguish between organizational goals (company objectives) and personal goals (career, recognition, ease).

### Behaviors and Habits
How does this persona currently solve the problem? What tools do they use? How do they discover, evaluate, and adopt products? What is their typical workflow?

### Pain Points and Frustrations
What makes their current situation difficult? What do they complain about? What causes them to seek alternatives? What do they tolerate but hate?

### Context and Environment
When, where, and how do they use the product? Desktop vs mobile, office vs remote, solo vs team? What constraints do they operate under? What is their technical proficiency?

### Representative Quote
A real quote from research that captures their essence.
"I don't need more data — I need someone to tell me what it means."

## Narrative Development

### Day-in-the-Life
Write a short narrative describing a typical day from the persona's perspective. Include their context, challenges, and how the product fits into their routine. Use first or third person consistently. Base on research data, not imagination. Make it vivid enough that team members feel they know this person.

### Scenario Writing
Develop specific scenarios where the persona interacts with the product. Before scenario: how they solve the problem today (pain). After scenario: how the product improves their situation (gain). Edge case scenarios: how they handle errors, limitations, outages.

## Persona Review Workshops

### Preparation
Print persona posters with name, photo, and key attributes. Prepare research evidence backing each persona attribute. Invite cross-functional team (product, design, eng, sales, support). Prepare questions: Is this persona recognizable? Does the evidence support the profile? Are we designing for the right primary persona?

### Facilitation
Present each persona with supporting research evidence. Ask: Who do you know who is like this? Does this ring true from your experience? What's missing or inaccurate? Do we have enough data to validate this persona? Discuss disagreements using research data as arbiter, not opinion.

### Validation
Rate confidence in each persona attribute: strong (multiple sources agree), moderate (some evidence), weak (thin data, needs more research). Mark low-confidence attributes for further validation. Plan follow-up research to fill gaps.

## Key Points
Personas are hypotheses, not facts — validate with continuous research.
Primary persona gets design priority; secondary gets accommodation.
Anti-persona is as important as primary — it prevents scope creep.
Personas without data are stereotypes — always cite research sources.
A persona is only useful if teams reference it in decisions.
Update personas annually or when market conditions shift significantly.
