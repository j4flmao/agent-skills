# GTM Strategy Playbook

## Overview
The GTM Strategy Playbook is a comprehensive guide to designing go-to-market strategies for product launches. It covers market analysis, ICP definition, competitive positioning, channel strategy, launch tiering, and strategic decision frameworks. A well-designed GTM strategy aligns product value with market opportunity and channels resources toward the highest-impact activities.

## Strategic Foundations

### What is a GTM Strategy?
A go-to-market strategy is the plan for delivering a product to the right customers through the right channels with the right messaging. It answers: who are we selling to? (target market and ICP), what are we selling? (product and positioning), how will we reach them? (channels and sales model), how will we convince them? (messaging and proof points), and how will we measure success? (metrics and targets).

A GTM strategy is not a marketing plan. A marketing plan executes the GTM strategy. The strategy defines what to do and why; the plan defines how and when. A GTM strategy precedes the marketing plan and is more foundational.

### Elements of a GTM Strategy
Market analysis: total addressable market (TAM), serviceable addressable market (SAM), serviceable obtainable market (SOM). Competitive landscape (direct, indirect, and emerging competitors). Market trends and inflection points.

Target customer: ICP definition (firmographic, demographic, behavioral). Persona development (buyer, influencer, user, champion). Segment prioritization (which segment to go after first).

Value proposition: positioning statement. Key benefits with quantified outcomes. Differentiation vs. alternatives. Proof points (data, case studies, testimonials).

Sales and distribution model: direct sales, self-serve, channel partners, OEM, marketplace. Pricing model (subscription, usage-based, perpetual, hybrid). Customer acquisition strategy.

Channel strategy: primary channel, secondary channels, organic channels. Channel-specific messaging and assets. Channel integration and attribution.

Launch plan: tier classification (T1, T2, T3). Timeline and milestones. Resource allocation. Success metrics.

### When to Create a GTM Strategy
New product launch: entering a market with a new product. No existing GTM motion. Requires full strategy development.

New market entry: existing product entering a new geographic market, vertical, or customer segment. Requires adaptation of existing GTM for the new context.

Major product expansion: significant new feature or platform expansion that changes the value proposition or target customer. Requires strategy refresh.

Pivot or repositioning: product has been repositioned for a different market or problem. Requires full strategy redevelopment.

Annual planning: refresh GTM strategy annually based on market changes, competitive dynamics, and performance data. Strategy evolves; it is not static.

## Market Analysis

### TAM, SAM, SOM Framework
Total Addressable Market (TAM): the total revenue opportunity if the product achieved 100% market share. Top-down: total market revenue from industry reports. Bottom-up: number of potential customers × average revenue per customer. TAM should be directionally accurate, not precisely calculated.

Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM): the portion of TAM that the product can serve given its capabilities, geography, and target segments. SAM = TAM × segments reachable with current product.

Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM): the portion of SAM that can realistically be captured given competition, resources, and time horizon. SOM = SAM × realistic market share (typically 1-10% for early-stage, 10-30% for mature products). SOM is the basis for revenue forecasting.

### Competitive Landscape Analysis
Direct competitors: products that solve the same problem for the same customer segment. Analyze: market share, growth rate, pricing, positioning, strengths, weaknesses. Use competitive matrix: features, pricing, target market, distribution channels, funding.

Indirect competitors: products that solve a different problem but compete for the same budget or attention. Example: a project management tool competes indirectly with communication tools (Slack) and documentation tools (Confluence) for the team productivity budget.

Emerging competitors: startups or new entrants that may become direct competitors in 12-24 months. Monitor: funding, team, product launches, customer traction, press coverage.

Substitute solutions: how the customer solves the problem today without any product. Manual processes, spreadsheets, email, paper, outsourcing. The substitute is often the biggest competitor because it has zero cost and zero switching friction.

### Market Trend Analysis
Technology trends: new technologies that enable product improvements or create new market opportunities. AI/ML, cloud infrastructure, mobile, API ecosystems. Evaluate: maturity, adoption rate, competitive implications.

Customer behavior trends: changing customer expectations, buying preferences, and usage patterns. Remote work, self-serve buying, subscription fatigue, privacy awareness. Evaluate: direction, magnitude, impact on GTM strategy.

Regulatory trends: new regulations that create requirements or constraints. GDPR, CCPA, industry-specific regulations (HIPAA, SOC2, FINRA). Evaluate: timeline, compliance cost, competitive advantage for compliant products.

Economic trends: macroeconomic factors affecting customer budgets and buying decisions. Recession, inflation, industry growth/decline, venture capital availability. Evaluate: impact on deal size, sales cycle, buyer urgency.

### Market Sizing Methods
Top-down: start with total market size from industry reports. Apply filters for geography, segment, product category. Multiply by market share assumptions. Fast but imprecise. Best for investor communication and strategic framing.

Bottom-up: start with unit economics. Number of customers this sales team can reach. Average deal size and conversion rate. Growth rate based on channel capacity. More accurate but requires detailed operational data. Best for resource planning and revenue forecasting.

Value theory: estimate the economic value the product delivers to customers. If the product saves a team 20 hours per week at $100/hour burden cost, the value is $2,000/week or ~$100K/year. Price as a fraction of value delivered. Best for premium or enterprise products.

## ICP and Persona Development

### ICP Definition Depth
Firmographic level: company size (employees, revenue segment), industry (vertical, sub-vertical), geography (region, country, metro), growth stage (startup, growth, mature, declining), funding (bootstrapped, venture-backed, public), technology stack (compatible technologies).

Demographic level: role title (exact titles that map to the persona), seniority (individual contributor, manager, director, VP, C-suite), functional area (marketing, engineering, product, sales, operations), decision authority (budget holder, recommender, influencer, end user), technical sophistication (low, medium, high).

Behavioral level: current solution (competitor, manual process, custom build), satisfaction with current solution (satisfied, somewhat satisfied, dissatisfied, actively looking), purchase triggers (specific events that start a buying process), budget availability (allocated, unallocated, no budget), buying process (self-serve, sales-assisted, procurement-driven), purchase timeline (immediate, this quarter, this year, no timeline).

Psychographic level: priorities and goals (what they want to achieve professionally), fears and frustrations (what keeps them up at night), values and beliefs (what they care about in a vendor), decision criteria (how they evaluate alternatives), information sources (where they get advice and recommendations).

### ICP Validation Methods
Customer data analysis: segment existing customers by firmographics and behavior. Calculate retention, LTV, expansion rate, NPS per segment. Identify which segments have best product-market fit. Use as ICP baseline.

Customer interviews: interview 10-20 customers who match the hypothesized ICP. Validate pain points: do they really have this problem? How important is it? Validate value proposition: does our solution resonate? How much would they pay? Validate purchase process: how would they buy? Who else is involved?

Win/loss analysis: analyze won and lost deals. What segments win most? What segments lose most? Why do they win or lose? Win/loss data often reveals that the actual ICP differs from the assumed ICP.

Market research: survey target market. Use third-party panels or LinkedIn targeting. Validate problem prevalence, willingness to pay, and purchase intent. Quantitative validation complements qualitative interviews.

### Persona Development
Buyer persona: the person who makes the purchase decision. Holds budget authority. Cares about ROI, business impact, risk. Needs business case, references, competitive comparison.

Influencer persona: the person who shapes the purchase decision. Domain expert or trusted advisor. Cares about functionality, technical fit, implementation. Needs product specifications, technical validation, demo.

User persona: the person who will use the product daily. Cares about usability, workflow integration, productivity impact. Needs user experience, onboarding, support.

Champion persona: the person who advocates for the product internally. Usually a user or influencer who believes strongly in the solution. Cares about solving their problem and getting organizational buy-in.

For each persona: name (memorable), role and seniority, goals and priorities, pain points and frustrations, decision criteria, information sources, GTM messaging angle.

## Competitive Positioning

### Positioning Statement Structure
For {target customer} who {have a specific need}, {our product} is a {category} that {provides a key benefit}. Unlike {competitor or alternative}, {our product} {key differentiator}.

Each element must be specific and validated:
- Target customer: the specific ICP segment, not "everyone."
- Specific need: the pain point or job-to-be-done that triggers purchase.
- Category: the market category the customer uses to understand the product.
- Key benefit: the primary outcome the customer gets.
- Competitor/alternative: what the customer would use instead.
- Differentiator: what makes the product uniquely able to deliver the benefit.

### April Dunford's Positioning Process
Step 1 — Competitive alternatives: who does the customer compare you to? List direct competitors, indirect competitors, and substitutes. Determining the competitive set is the most important positioning decision because it defines the context.

Step 2 — Unique attributes: what can the product do that alternatives cannot? List features, capabilities, and experiences unique to the product. These are the raw materials for differentiation.

Step 3 — Value: for each unique attribute, what is the customer impact? Attribute + customer impact = value. Quantify the value where possible: "automated reporting saves 10 hours per week."

Step 4 — Target market: who cares most about this value? The market segment that values these unique attributes most highly. This is the beachhead segment.

Step 5 — Category: what category makes the value clear to the target market? The right category helps customers understand what the product is and why it matters. Sometimes creating a new category is the right move; sometimes positioning within an existing category is better.

Step 6 — Competition: who else is in this category? The competitive frame should make the product look uniquely valuable. Choose competitors that highlight the product's advantages.

Step 7 — Positioning statement: synthesize into a clear, compelling statement. Test with target customers. Iterate based on feedback.

### Competitive Differentiation Types
Feature differentiation: the product has capabilities competitors lack. Most common but easiest to copy. Sustainable only with continuous innovation.

Performance differentiation: the product delivers better outcomes (faster, cheaper, more reliable, higher quality). Measurable and defensible with data.

Experience differentiation: the product is easier to use, more enjoyable, or more intuitive. Harder to copy than features but harder to quantify.

Ecosystem differentiation: the product integrates with other tools the customer already uses. Creates switching costs and network effects.

Business model differentiation: different pricing, deployment, or purchasing model. Subscription vs. perpetual, self-serve vs. sales-led, cloud vs. on-premise.

### Competitive Positioning Mistakes
Feature parity trap: positioning against competitors feature-by-feature implies the products are comparable and the decision is about features. Leads to price competition. Instead, compete on value, not features.

Undifferentiated positioning: claiming to be "the best" or "the most innovative" without specific, validated differentiation. Customers cannot distinguish the product from alternatives. Instead, find a specific, meaningful difference.

Competitor fixation: obsessing over competitors instead of customers. Results in copycat features and reactive strategy. Instead, focus on customer needs and use competitive intelligence to inform, not drive, strategy.

Changing positioning too often: repositioning quarterly based on competitive moves. Confuses the market and prevents positioning from sticking. Instead, set positioning annually and adjust only based on customer feedback, not competitive noise.

## Channel Strategy

### Channel Types

Direct sales: internal sales team selling to customers. Best for high ACV ($50K+), complex products, long sales cycles, consultative selling. Requires: SDRs, account executives, sales engineers, sales enablement.

Inside sales: remote sales team selling by phone, email, and video. Best for mid-market ACV ($10-50K), moderately complex products. Requires: SDRs, inside sales reps, demo capability.

Self-serve: customers sign up and buy without human interaction. Best for low ACV (<$5K), simple products, high volume. Requires: product-led growth, in-app conversion, automated onboarding.

Channel partners: resellers, VARs, system integrators, agencies. Best for geographic expansion, vertical markets, or complement to direct sales. Requires: partner program, enablement, deal registration, partner portal.

Marketplaces: app stores, cloud marketplaces (AWS, GCP, Salesforce AppExchange). Best for products that complement existing platforms. Requires: marketplace listing, co-sell relationship, integration.

Strategic partnerships: technology partnerships, integration partnerships, go-to-market partnerships with non-competing products. Best for co-selling, co-marketing, and expanding reach. Requires: partner identification, agreement, joint execution.

### Channel Selection Criteria
Reach to ICP: does this channel actually reach the target customer? In-app messaging reaches existing users. Content marketing reaches search-driven buyers. Sales reaches enterprise buyers. Channel must match ICP.

Cost efficiency: what is the cost per customer acquired through this channel? Include people, technology, and program costs. Compare across channels using CAC. Lower CAC is better, but consider LTV and scale.

Scalability: can this channel grow with the business without proportional cost increase? Content marketing and self-serve scale well. Direct sales scales linearly with headcount.

Time to revenue: how quickly does this channel convert? Sales (weeks to months), content marketing (months), self-serve (days to weeks). Match channel to revenue timing needs.

Fit with product: does this channel showcase the product effectively? Some products need demos (sales), some need trial (self-serve), some need reference (content).

### Channel Mix Strategy
Top-of-funnel channels: generate awareness and attract potential customers. Content marketing, SEO, paid advertising, social media, PR, events, webinars. Metrics: reach, impressions, traffic, leads.

Mid-funnel channels: nurture prospects and build consideration. Email marketing, retargeting, content offers, webinars, case studies, demo requests. Metrics: engagement, lead quality, conversion to opportunity.

Bottom-of-funnel channels: convert prospects to customers. Sales outreach, product trial, proposal, negotiation, reference calls, contract. Metrics: opportunity-to-close rate, deal size, sales cycle length.

Integrated channel strategy: top-funnel feeds mid-funnel feeds bottom-funnel. Channels are not independent — they work together as a system. Attribution models should account for multi-touch conversion paths.

### Channel Economics
Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC): total channel cost / customers acquired. Include people, technology, and program costs. Track CAC by channel and by segment. Target: CAC decreases over time as channel efficiency improves.

Payback period: CAC / monthly revenue per customer. Months to recover acquisition cost. Target: <12 months for most businesses. Longer payback periods require more working capital.

Channel capacity: maximum customers a channel can deliver per period. Sales team: deals per rep × number of reps. Self-serve: traffic × conversion rate. Content: content pieces × traffic per piece. Channel capacity determines growth ceiling.

## Launch Tiering

### Tier Classification Framework
T1 — Major launch: new product, major platform release, entry into new market. Full GTM campaign across all channels. Dedicated team (3-5 people). 8-12 week planning cycle. Board-level visibility. Budget: significant (dedicated GTM budget).

T2 — Significant launch: major feature, significant product improvement, expansion to adjacent segment. Targeted campaign to existing users and ICP prospects. GTM lead + cross-functional support. 3-5 week planning cycle. Department-level visibility. Budget: moderate.

T3 — Minor launch: minor feature, bug fix, UI improvement, performance enhancement. Lightweight announcement. Product manager + comms support. 1-2 week planning cycle. Team-level visibility. Budget: minimal.

### Tier Decision Criteria
Strategic importance: is this launch critical to company strategy, revenue targets, or competitive position? T1 if yes.

Customer impact: will this launch significantly change how customers use the product or the value they receive? T1-T2 if yes.

Scope of change: how much of the product is changing? New product (T1), major feature (T2), minor improvement (T3).

Market timing: is there a market window or competitive imperative that demands a strong launch? T1 if timing is critical.

Resource availability: does the team have the resources to execute a T1 launch? If not, either reduce scope or delay until resources are available.

### Launch Resource Allocation
T1 resource requirements: dedicated GTM lead (1 FTE), product marketing (1 FTE), content marketing (1 FTE), demand generation (1 FTE), sales enablement (0.5 FTE), customer marketing (0.5 FTE), design (0.5 FTE), engineering support (0.25 FTE). Total: ~5-6 FTE for 8-12 weeks.

T2 resource requirements: GTM lead (can be product manager), product marketing (0.5 FTE), content (0.25 FTE), demand gen (0.25 FTE), sales enablement (0.25 FTE). Total: ~1.5-2 FTE for 3-5 weeks.

T3 resource requirements: product manager (owns the launch), comms (0.1 FTE), engineering (as needed). Total: ~0.2-0.5 FTE for 1-2 weeks.

## GTM Metrics and KPIs

### Pre-Launch Metrics
ICP validation: percentage of existing customers matching ICP. Interview feedback confirming pain points and value proposition. Win/loss data supporting ICP hypothesis.

Messaging effectiveness: message recall and resonance scores from customer testing. A/B test results on messaging variants. Clarity score (target customers can explain the product in their own words after hearing the messaging).

Channel readiness: assets created per channel. Channel testing results (email deliverability, ad platform approval, landing page conversion). Sales team training completion rate.

### Launch Day Metrics
Awareness: website traffic (unique visitors, page views), press coverage (articles, mentions, reach), social media (impressions, engagement, sentiment), email open and click rates, ad impressions and CTR.

Acquisition: signups or trial starts (total and by channel), activation rate (percentage of signups completing onboarding), cost per acquisition (by channel), traffic-to-signup conversion rate.

Engagement: feature adoption rate (percentage of users who try the new product/feature), time spent in new product, key actions completed, NPS or satisfaction score.

### Post-Launch Metrics
Adoption: weekly active users of the new product/feature, feature stickiness (return rate), depth of use (actions per user), time to first value.

Retention: day 7, day 30, day 90 retention for new product users, churn rate, product-specific engagement trends.

Revenue: new revenue attributed to the launch, expansion revenue from existing customers, pipeline influenced, conversion rate, average deal size.

Customer feedback: NPS (by segment, by time since launch), CSAT surveys at key touchpoints, support ticket volume and themes, feature requests and bug reports.

### GTM ROA Analysis
Revenue attributed to launch: incremental revenue directly attributable to the GTM campaign. Compare to baseline (pre-launch revenue trend). Use attribution modeling (first-touch, last-touch, multi-touch).

GTM campaign cost: total cost of people, programs, and technology for the launch. Include internal costs (FTE time capitalized) and external costs (agencies, tools, ads).

Return on GTM investment: (incremental revenue - campaign cost) / campaign cost. Measure at D90 and D180. Target: > 3x return on GTM investment.

## Strategic Decision Frameworks

### Beachhead Selection (Where to Launch First)
Select the initial market segment to focus on. Criteria: strongest product-market fit (which segment most needs the product?), easiest to reach (which segment is accessible through existing channels?), highest willingness to pay (which segment values the solution most?), fastest sales cycle (which segment buys fastest?), reference value (which segment has the most influential references?).

Beachhead strategy: dominate one small segment before expanding. Creates proof points, reference customers, and product refinement. Expansion to adjacent segments is easier with a strong beachhead.

### Build vs. Buy vs. Partner for Channels
Build: develop channel capability internally. Best for core channels that provide competitive advantage. Requires investment in people, processes, and systems.

Buy: acquire a company with channel capability. Best for rapid expansion into new geographies or segments. Requires integration and culture alignment.

Partner: leverage existing channel partners. Best for non-core channels or when partners have existing customer relationships. Requires partner program, enablement, and management.

### Go/No-Go Decision Criteria
Before launch day, make a formal go/no-go decision based on:

Product readiness: are all critical features complete and tested? Are known bugs acceptable for launch? Is performance acceptable? Is documentation complete?

Customer readiness: has beta feedback been incorporated? Are reference customers ready? Is support trained to handle questions?

Market readiness: is the messaging and positioning validated? Are channel assets complete? Is the sales team enabled? Is the launch sequence tested?

Business readiness: are success metrics defined and measurable? Are targets set? Is the launch team staffed and ready?

Go/no-go decision rule: all readiness criteria must be green. If any is yellow, proceed with contingency. If any is red, delay. Never launch with red criteria — it is better to delay than to launch poorly.

### Post-Launch Pivot or Persevere
At D30, D60, and D90 reviews, assess performance against success criteria. If metrics are on track: persevere with current strategy, optimize channels and messaging, extend to secondary segments.

If metrics are below target but direction is positive: adjust strategy based on data (channel reallocation, messaging refinement, segment focus), set a checkpoint for the next review period.

If metrics are significantly below target and direction is flat or negative: consider pivot (new segment, new positioning, new channels), delay further investment until strategy is refined, or shut down the launch and redirect resources.

## Key Points
- GTM strategy defines who, what, how, and why for bringing a product to market. It precedes the marketing plan.
- Validate ICP with customer data and interviews — untested ICP assumptions are the most common GTM failure.
- Positioning determines how customers perceive the product relative to alternatives — invest in getting it right.
- Channel strategy must match ICP: meet customers where they are, not where it is easiest to execute.
- Launch tier honestly: T1 needs full resources, T3 should not consume disproportionate resources.
- Go/no-go decisions must be based on readiness data, not schedule pressure.
- Post-launch optimization is where most value is captured — allocate 60% of GTM resources to post-launch.
- Measure GTM performance with pre-launch, launch day, and post-launch metrics across awareness, acquisition, engagement, retention, and revenue.
- Beachhead strategy: dominate one segment before expanding.
- Competitor fixation is a trap — focus on customers and use competitive intelligence to inform, not drive, strategy.
