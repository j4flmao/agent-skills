# GTM Launch Execution Playbook

## Overview
GTM launch execution is the operational discipline of bringing a product to market — turning strategy into action across channels, teams, and timelines. This reference covers launch planning, cross-functional coordination, channel execution, monitoring, and post-launch optimization. While strategy defines what to do and why, execution defines how and when.

## Launch Planning

### Working Backward from Launch Day
Launch planning starts with the launch date and works backward to identify all activities, dependencies, and milestones. The planning horizon depends on launch tier:

T1 launch: 8-12 weeks of pre-launch planning. Activities include: beta program, ICP validation, messaging testing, asset production, channel preparation, press and analyst outreach, internal enablement, sales training, support training.

T2 launch: 3-5 weeks of pre-launch planning. Activities include: asset production, email campaign preparation, in-app messaging configuration, blog post writing, sales enablement brief, internal announcement.

T3 launch: 1-2 weeks of pre-launch planning. Activities include: in-app notification setup, changelog entry, social media post, internal communication.

### Launch Plan Components
Launch plan document includes: product overview (what is being launched, why it matters), target audience (ICP, personas, segments), launch tier (T1, T2, T3 with rationale), channels (primary, secondary, organic per channel plan), messaging (positioning statement, messaging hierarchy, channel variants), assets (list of all deliverables with owners and deadlines), timeline (weekly milestones with dependencies and owners), metrics (success criteria with targets), risks (identified risks with mitigations), team (roles and responsibilities).

### Dependency Mapping
Identify all dependencies between activities and teams. Common dependencies:

Product on engineering: feature completion, performance, stability, documentation.

Marketing on product: messaging validation requires beta feedback, demo video requires working product, screenshots require final UI.

Sales on marketing: sales training requires finalized messaging and positioning, sales collateral requires approved assets and case studies.

Support on product: support training requires documentation and known issues list, support readiness requires tested workflows and FAQ.

External on internal: press outreach requires approved messaging and embargo agreement, analyst briefings require product demonstration and business context.

For each dependency, identify: what is needed, who provides it, who needs it, the deadline, and contingency if the dependency is delayed. Map dependencies on the timeline to ensure critical path activities are identified and managed.

### Buffer Planning
Include buffer time for unexpected delays. Buffer allocation: 20% of total planning time for T1 launches, 15% for T2, 10% for T3.

Buffer placement: between major milestones (beta close to launch preparation), before the final go/no-go decision (T-2 weeks), and at the end of the timeline (to absorb delays without moving launch day).

Buffer management: track buffer consumption weekly. If buffer is being consumed faster than planned, escalate to leadership. Major delays may require moving the launch date.

## Cross-Functional Team Coordination

### GTM Team Structure
GTM lead: owns the launch plan, coordinates cross-functional team, makes go/no-go recommendation, reports to leadership. Typically product marketing manager or senior product manager.

Core team members: product manager (product readiness, feature prioritization, beta feedback), product marketing (messaging, positioning, competitive analysis, sales enablement), demand generation (channel execution, campaign management, lead generation), content marketing (blog posts, case studies, white papers, videos), sales enablement (sales training, collateral, demo script), customer marketing (beta program, customer references, launch comms to existing customers), design (creative assets: landing pages, emails, social graphics, sales deck), engineering (technical readiness, performance, bug fixes, documentation), support (support readiness, training, FAQ, known issues).

Supporting team members: PR/communications (press outreach, analyst relations, launch announcements), partnerships (partner enablement, co-marketing, co-selling), legal (messaging review, competitive claims, beta agreements), analytics (dashboard setup, metric tracking, reporting).

### Communication Cadence
Weekly GTM standup: 30 minutes, all core team members. Agenda: progress against timeline, blocking issues, decisions needed, upcoming milestones. Standing meeting every week at same time for the duration of the launch.

Daily during launch week: 15-minute standup. Agenda: previous day results, today's activities, issues and escalations. High-intensity period requires daily coordination.

Bi-weekly leadership update: 15 minutes with exec sponsor. Agenda: overall progress, key metrics (if available), risks and issues, go/no-go recommendation (as launch approaches). Keep execs informed, not surprised.

### Decision Rights
GTM lead decides: launch timeline and milestone adjustments within buffer, channel mix and budget allocation within approved budget, messaging within approved positioning framework, go/no-go recommendation (to leadership).

Product manager decides: feature scope for launch, priority of beta feedback items, bug fix vs. ship decisions, launch readiness from product perspective.

Marketing lead decides: channel execution details, creative direction within brand guidelines, campaign budget allocation, vendor selection for marketing services.

Sales leader decides: sales team readiness criteria, sales incentive structure, enablement approach, account targeting.

Leadership (VP/CPO) decides: go/no-go decision, launch date changes, major scope changes, additional budget requests, escalation resolution.

### Escalation Path
Team-level escalation: unresolved dependencies, resource constraints, timeline risks. Escalate to GTM lead first for resolution within team.

Cross-functional escalation: conflicting priorities between teams, resource contention, scope disagreements. Escalate to GTM lead and relevant functional leaders for resolution.

Executive escalation: launch date change required, significant budget increase needed, major scope reduction, organizational blockers. Escalate to executive sponsor for decision.

## Asset Production

### Asset Inventory by Launch Tier
T1 launch assets: landing page, email sequence (3-5 emails), blog post (1-2), case study (1-2), white paper or guide, product demo video, sales deck, one-pager, comparison sheet, FAQ, press release, media kit, social media kit (5-10 posts), in-app messaging (announcement, tooltips, banners), sales enablement guide, support guide.

T2 launch assets: landing page or product page update, email (1-2), blog post (1), in-app announcement, social media (2-3 posts), sales enablement summary, support FAQ update.

T3 launch assets: in-app notification, changelog entry, social media post (1), internal announcement.

### Asset Development Process
Brief: asset owner creates a brief: purpose, audience, key message, format, deadline, dependencies. Review by GTM lead for alignment.

First draft: asset owner creates first draft. Content assets go through product marketing review. Design assets go through design review.

Review and revision: relevant stakeholders review. Product accuracy by PM. Messaging alignment by product marketing. Brand compliance by design. Legal review as needed.

Approval: final review by GTM lead. Sign-off before publication or distribution.

Distribution: asset is published on the appropriate channel. Added to asset library for ongoing use.

### Asset Production Timeline
T-8 to T-12 weeks (T1 only): foundational assets begin: white paper, case study interviews, demo video script.

T-4 to T-6 weeks: major asset production: landing page, email sequence, sales deck, one-pager, comparison sheet, media kit.

T-2 to T-4 weeks: final asset production: blog posts, social media kit, in-app messaging, support guide, FAQ. Assets are finalized and approved.

T-1 to T-2 weeks: asset distribution setup: email sequences scheduled, landing page published (unlisted), in-app messaging configured, ads created and approved.

Launch day: assets go live: landing page published, emails sent, blog posted, social posted, in-app announcement activated.

### Asset Quality Standards
Messaging consistency: every asset communicates the same core value proposition. Headlines align with positioning statement. Key benefits are consistent. Differentiation is clear.

Brand compliance: visual assets follow brand guidelines (colors, fonts, spacing, logo usage). Tone of voice is consistent with brand personality. All assets pass brand review.

Accuracy: product claims are tested and verified. Pricing is current. Features are accurate. Screenshots show the final UI. Technical specifications are correct.

Legal compliance: claims are substantiated. Competitor comparisons are factual. Testimonials are approved for use. Privacy and consent requirements are met.

## Channel Execution

### Email Campaign Execution
Campaign structure: welcome or announcement email (launch day), benefit-focused follow-up (day 2-3), social proof and case studies (day 5-7), feature deep dive (day 10-14), final reminder or offer expiration (day 14-21).

Segmentation: send to ICP-matched segments first. Existing customers (segment by usage of related features). Prospects (segment by engagement level). Lapsed users (re-engagement angle). Personalize subject line and body per segment.

Testing: A/B test subject lines (open rate). A/B test CTA (click rate). A/B test body copy (conversion rate). Test on small sample (10-20%) before full send.

Deliverability: warm up new domains or IPs before launch. Authenticate with SPF, DKIM, DMARC. Monitor bounce rate (<2%) and spam complaint rate (<0.1%). Clean list of inactive or invalid addresses before launch.

### In-App Messaging Execution
Announcement types: modal (attention-grabbing, best for T1 launches), banner (persistent but less intrusive, best for T2), tooltip (contextual, best for feature-specific T2/T3), what's new feed (in-app changelog, always available).

Timing: announce on first visit after launch. For existing users: show announcement on next app open. For new users: incorporate into onboarding flow. Sequence: announcement → tooltip on new feature → education on how to use it.

Targeting: all users for broad announcements. Segment by plan (free users see different messaging than paid), role (admins see setup information, users see usage information), usage (heavy users of related features get more detail), tenure (new users get onboarding-focused messaging, existing users get upgrade messaging).

Measurement: views, clicks, dismissals, feature adoption (did users who saw the announcement use the feature?), time-to-first-action.

### Content Marketing Execution
Blog post: launch announcement article. Structure: problem (what was hard before), solution (what the new product/feature enables), proof (metrics, case study, customer quote), CTA (try it, learn more). Publish on company blog. Distribute through email, social, and community.

Case study: customer success story. Structure: customer background (who they are, what they do), challenge (what problem they faced), solution (how they used the product), results (quantified outcomes), customer quote. Develop during beta program. Publish 2-4 weeks after launch.

Product demo video: short video (2-5 minutes) showing the product in action. Structure: problem intro, product walkthrough, key features, outcomes. Best for T1 launches. Use for landing page, sales, and social.

Technical content: developer docs, API reference, integration guides. For developer-facing products or features. Publish before launch day so developers can start building.

### Sales Enablement Execution
Sales training: session covering product overview, key features, target ICP, positioning and messaging, competitive differentiation, pricing and packaging, objection handling, demo script, qualification criteria. Deliver 2-4 weeks before launch. Record for ongoing reference.

Sales collateral: sales deck (presentation for prospects), one-pager (summary for quick reference), comparison sheet (vs. top competitors), case study (customer proof point), demo script (structured walkthrough), pricing sheet, FAQ (common questions and answers), battle card (competitor-specific responses). Deliver with sales training.

Demo environment: working demo instance for sales team. Pre-configured with sample data showing the product at its best. Available for demo calls and trial accounts.

### PR and Communications Execution
Press preparation: press release (announcement, quotes, availability), media kit (screenshots, logos, exec photos, company description, product fact sheet), executive briefing (talking points, Q&A preparation, key messages). Prepare 4-6 weeks before launch.

Press outreach: identify target journalists and analysts covering the product category, company stage, and competitive set. Personalize outreach per journalist. Offer early access or exclusive embargo. Follow up respectfully.

Analyst relations: brief industry analysts on the launch before public announcement. Provide analyst briefing with product demo, business context, and market positioning. Request analyst feedback on positioning.

Launch day PR: press release on wire (if applicable), direct pitches to target journalists, social media announcement from company and executive accounts, blog post on company blog.

## Beta Program Execution

### Beta Program Phases
Recruitment phase (2-3 weeks): define qualification criteria, identify candidates from existing customers and waitlist, reach out with personalized invitation, screen candidates against criteria, select participants, send welcome package.

Active phase (4-8 weeks): onboarding session (walkthrough, expectations, feedback process), weekly feedback survey, bi-weekly check-in call (product manager or designer), NPS survey at midpoint and end, bug tracking and prioritization, feature request collection and review.

Close phase (1-2 weeks): final retrospective call, NPS survey, reference call request, incentive delivery, transition plan (what happens after beta), thank-you communication.

### Beta Participant Management
Communication: weekly email with updates, known issues, and upcoming changes. Dedicated Slack channel or community for real-time discussion. Office hours (weekly open Q&A with product team).

Feedback collection: structured feedback (weekly survey: what worked, what did not, what is missing). Unstructured feedback (Slack, email, calls: capture everything, analyze for themes). Usage analytics (track feature adoption, engagement, drop-off, NPS).

Expectation management: set clear expectations at onboarding: beta is not final quality, feedback is valued, timeline for fixes may vary, participation is voluntary, incentive is provided. Manage expectations throughout to prevent frustration.

### Beta Feedback Integration
Feedback prioritization: collect all feedback. Categorize by type (bug, missing feature, UX improvement, performance, documentation). Prioritize by frequency (how many users reported), severity (how critical), effort (how hard to fix), and impact on launch decision.

Feedback-to-action loop: for each piece of feedback, communicate the action taken: fixed (bug resolved), planned (will be addressed post-launch), considered (evaluating for future), declined (not aligned with product direction). Closing the loop builds trust and encourages continued feedback.

Go/no-go input: beta feedback is a key input to the go/no-go decision. Criteria: critical bugs resolved (none blocking launch), NPS > 30 (positive sentiment), feature adoption meets target (users are using the product), reference customers available (at least 2-3 willing to be public references).

## Launch Day Execution

### Launch Sequence
Time T (announcement time): press release goes live, blog post published, social media announced, email sent to all segments, in-app announcement activated, landing page published, ad campaigns activated.

T+1 hour: monitor all channels for issues (broken links, errors, unexpected behavior). Check social media for sentiment and questions. Begin responding to press inquiries.

T+4 hours: review initial metrics: website traffic, signups, activation, support volume. Compare to targets. Identify any anomalies requiring immediate attention.

T+24 hours: full day 1 review: all channel metrics, press coverage roundup, social sentiment analysis, support ticket analysis, early user feedback. Adjust channel mix if needed.

T+72 hours: early trend analysis: signup trend, activation rate, feature adoption, support themes. Compare to D7 targets. Identify optimization opportunities.

### Launch Day Monitoring
Real-time dashboard: website traffic (users, sessions, by channel), signups (total, by channel), activation rate, support volume (total, by category), press coverage (articles, reach, sentiment), social metrics (impressions, engagement, sentiment), revenue (if applicable). Monitor every 2-4 hours on launch day.

Alert thresholds: signups below 50% of target (check channel performance), support volume above 3x baseline (check for critical issues), activation rate below 50% of target (check onboarding flow), negative social sentiment spike (check for backlash), site performance degradation (check engineering).

Response team: GTM lead (overall coordination), product manager (product questions, bug triage), marketing lead (channel optimization, press response), support lead (customer inquiries, escalation), engineering lead (performance, stability, bugs).

### Launch Day Decision Framework
Stay course: all metrics on track, no critical issues. Continue with planned execution.

Optimize: metrics below target but not critical. Adjust channel mix, refresh messaging, or increase spend. Do not change product.

Respond: critical issue detected (product bug, performance degradation, negative press). Activate response plan. Issue fix, communicate internally and externally as appropriate.

Pause: major product issue or unexpected event requiring immediate stop. Pause campaigns, pull ads, issue hold on communications. Fix issue before resuming.

## Post-Launch Optimization

### Week 1-2: Stabilization
Objective: ensure launch is stable and metrics are trending in the right direction. Activities: fix critical bugs discovered during launch, optimize underperforming channels (adjust targeting, creative, budget), respond to press and analyst inquiries, activate customer references for sales enablement, review early feedback and prioritize quick wins.

Metrics focus: activation rate, feature adoption, support ticket volume and themes, NPS, early churn signal.

### Week 3-4: Optimization
Objective: optimize channel performance and messaging based on launch data. Activities: A/B test channel-specific messaging (landing page headlines, email subject lines, ad creative), reallocate budget to best-performing channels, publish additional content (case studies, technical posts, customer stories), extend reach to secondary segments if primary segment is performing well.

Metrics focus: conversion rate per channel, cost per acquisition, activation rate trend, feature adoption depth.

### Month 2: Extension
Objective: extend launch reach and deepen engagement. Activities: launch targeted campaigns for secondary ICP segments, develop additional sales enablement (battle cards, ROI calculator, competitive positioning), run webinar or event featuring the product, activate partners for co-selling or co-marketing.

Metrics focus: new segment acquisition, expansion revenue, retention rate, LTV.

### Month 3: Maturation
Objective: transition from launch mode to business-as-usual. Activities: D90 comprehensive review, document lessons learned, hand off ongoing optimization to product and growth teams, sunset launch-specific campaigns and assets, incorporate learnings into next launch plan.

Metrics focus: full performance against success criteria, retention and LTV, competitive impact, customer satisfaction.

### Post-Launch Channel Optimization
Email: A/B test subject lines, send times, personalization. Segment further based on launch engagement. Extend sequence for non-responders. Test content formats (case study vs. feature deep dive vs. offer).

In-app: optimize announcement placement, timing, and targeting based on engagement data. Add contextual tooltips for feature discovery. Test different calls to action.

Content: promote best-performing content through paid distribution. Update content based on customer feedback and questions. Create additional content for specific segments or use cases.

Sales: provide updated competitive intelligence based on launch experience. Refine demo script based on prospect feedback. Add new case studies and references.

### Competitive Response Monitoring
Monitor competitor reactions: competitive product announcements, pricing changes, messaging changes, marketing campaigns targeting the same ICP, analyst reports mentioning the launch.

Document competitive response: what did each competitor do? When? What does it mean for the product? What is the recommended response (counter-messaging, feature investment, pricing adjustment)?

Update competitive positioning: incorporate competitive responses into sales enablement (updated battle cards, messaging), product strategy (feature prioritization), and GTM strategy (channel and segment adjustments).

## Launch Review and Retrospective

### D30 Review
Scope: early performance check. Metrics: signups vs. target, activation rate, feature adoption, support volume and themes, NPS, early revenue if applicable. Qualitative: early customer feedback themes, press coverage quality, sales team feedback, competitive response.

Decision: continue with current strategy, adjust channels/messaging, address critical issues, plan D60 review with refined approach.

### D60 Review
Scope: mid-point performance assessment. Metrics: D30 retention, feature stickiness, conversion rate, revenue (pipeline, closed deals). Channel performance (CAC by channel, conversion by channel). Segment analysis (which segments are performing). Qualitative: customer interviews, sales team feedback, competitive response analysis.

Decision: persist with strategy (optimize), pivot (new segment, positioning, or channels), invest more (increase budget, extend campaigns), or deprioritize (reduce investment, maintain).

### D90 Review
Scope: comprehensive performance evaluation. Metrics: D90 retention, LTV projection, revenue vs. target, CAC payback period, NPS trend, organic growth (referral, word-of-mouth). Channel ROI. Segment performance.

Lessons learned: what went well (replicate in future launches), what went poorly (avoid in future launches), what surprised us (unexpected outcomes), what would we do differently (process improvements).

Documentation: formal retrospective document. Include performance data, lessons learned, and recommendations for future GTM launches. Share with the organization.

### Lessons Learned Repository
Structure per launch: launch name, date, tier, GTM lead. What worked (channel execution, messaging, timing, team coordination). What did not work (underperforming channels, messaging misses, timeline issues, resource gaps). Key metrics (performance vs. targets, with context on why). Recommendations (specific changes for future launches: process, resourcing, timing, channels).

Use the repository as input for: next launch planning, GTM process improvement, team training and onboarding, annual GTM strategy.

## Key Points
- Plan backward from launch date with clear milestones, owners, and dependencies.
- Build 20% buffer into T1 launch timelines — delays are inevitable.
- Cross-functional GTM team requires clear roles, decision rights, and escalation paths.
- Asset production must start early enough for review and revision cycles.
- Launch day execution requires real-time monitoring, clear decision framework, and response team.
- Launch is the start, not the end — allocate 60% of resources to post-launch optimization.
- Post-launch phases: stabilization (week 1-2), optimization (week 3-4), extension (month 2), maturation (month 3).
- Conduct D30, D60, and D90 reviews with structured metrics and decision frameworks.
- Document lessons learned in a shared repository for continuous GTM process improvement.
- Monitor competitive response and update positioning, messaging, and enablement accordingly.
